The CFO's Lens

Equipment Financing as a Growth Lever — If Anyone Will Write the Loan

Tebe Williams · March 2026

Equipment financing has powered the physical economy for a century. The model is simple and it works: a lender advances capital against a piece of equipment, the borrower makes payments over time, and if something goes wrong, the lender takes back the asset and finds another buyer. A backhoe is a backhoe. A tractor is a tractor. The market for used construction equipment is deep, liquid, and well-understood.

Physical AI is breaking that model. And almost nobody in the lending industry is talking about it clearly yet.

The Software Problem Nobody Has Solved

When a bank underwrites an equipment loan, they are not just evaluating your creditworthiness. They are evaluating the asset itself. Specifically: If you default, can we take this thing back and sell it to someone else?

A John Deere tractor repossessed in Kansas can be shipped to a buyer in Brazil. The new owner doesn't need John Deere's permission to operate it. The machine and its capability travel together.

A Physical AI system doesn't work that way. The hardware and the intelligence are separate. The robot arm is the body. The trained model, the integration layer, the operational software — that's the brain. And the brain stays with the original developer. A lender who repossesses a Physical AI deployment may have hardware that is, in practical terms, inoperable without a licensing agreement, a software integration project, and a new training pipeline. The pool of buyers capable of taking that asset and doing something productive with it is small. In some cases, it may be one company: the original manufacturer.

IoT devices have faced a version of this problem too — they've never been widely equipment financed either. But the reason is different. A smart sensor or connected gateway costs a few hundred dollars. You don't need a loan to buy one. Physical AI systems are a different order of magnitude: purpose-built robots, autonomous vehicles, and intelligent industrial equipment routinely run into the hundreds of thousands of dollars per unit. That price point makes financing not just useful but necessary — and the collateral problem that was irrelevant for a cheap IoT device becomes a serious structural obstacle at that scale.

The asset-transferability assumption — the foundational logic of every equipment loan ever written — doesn't hold for software-dependent physical systems.

What's Actually Happening in the Market

The financing activity in AI right now is concentrated in two places that don't solve this problem.

The first is venture capital — equity rounds funding the development of Physical AI companies, not the deployment of their equipment at scale. The second is infrastructure financing: GPU-backed loans, data center construction debt, cloud hardware. These work because GPUs are commoditized. An H100 is an H100. CoreWeave raised over two billion dollars pledging GPU inventory as collateral precisely because there is a deep secondary market for standardized compute hardware. A purpose-built Physical AI deployment has no equivalent.

Specialty "story credit" lenders are stepping into the robotics space, but their approach — evaluating business potential and future cash flow rather than asset collateral — is closer to unsecured lending than traditional equipment finance. It works for smaller deployments. It doesn't scale to the capital requirements that Physical AI will generate as companies move from dozens of units to thousands.

The Opportunity Nobody Has Built Yet

Here is what makes this interesting: The collateral problem is real, but it is not permanent. And the market sitting on the other side of it is enormous.

Physical AI companies are not deploying one machine. They are deploying fleets — hundreds, then thousands, then tens of thousands of units as their customers scale. The revenue those units generate is often contracted, predictable, and tied directly to measurable operational outcomes. That's a cash flow profile that lenders understand. The problem is not the borrower's ability to repay. The problem is what happens to the asset if they don't.

Solving that problem requires new structures. Manufacturer buyback guarantees that give lenders a committed exit if a loan defaults. Software licensing agreements that travel with the hardware in a repossession scenario. Fleet-level financing structures that treat the deployment as a portfolio rather than underwriting each unit individually, spreading residual value risk across a large enough asset base to make the math work.

None of this is unprecedented. The auto industry figured out residual value risk decades ago. Aviation financing built entire frameworks around assets that are only valuable if they keep flying. The intellectual infrastructure exists. What doesn't exist yet is the equipment finance company that has applied it specifically to Physical AI.

That company will have an enormous first-mover advantage. The addressable market is every manufacturer, logistics operator, and industrial business that will be deploying Physical AI at scale over the next decade.

The financing gap is real. So is the opportunity.
