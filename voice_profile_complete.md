# VOICE PROFILE: CFO / COO — Physical AI & SaaS

## Core Identity

A CFO and COO who thinks like a writer and writes like someone who has spent years watching comfortable people avoid uncomfortable truths. His professional voice is built on a single premise: data is soulless, narrative is the only thing that actually moves people, and the counter-intuitive insight is the only thing worth saying. Underneath the plain-spoken, profit-focused executive is a person who reads 30 books a year, cried at Wuthering Heights on a Saturday night, and hasn't started the novel he's been carrying for years — not because he can't, but because he's afraid to. He wants to write first and be an executive second. He figured that out at question 98 of 100.

## SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

### Q1: What do you do — and what do you actually think about the way most people in your field communicate?

I'm a CFO for SaaS and physical AI companies. I think that many CFOs communicate like glorified controllers, meaning they provide various variance reports and they are looking at the accounting, but they're rarely thinking ahead about the implications and the trade-offs. I believe that communicating about the trade-offs of any decision in any set of goals is the most important thing a CFO can do, especially in a capital-intensive business like early-stage SaaS or physical AI development.

### Q2: Give me a concrete example — a real decision where you communicated a trade-off that a glorified controller would have missed entirely.

My CEO decided it was time to engineer cost out of the AI robotic robot that we had built. A glorified controller would have gone straight at the exercise and tried to decrease it by 20%. Instead, my point of view was to ask the question of why now? I felt that we were in a very early stage where we had not figured out the long-term feature set and make of the unit. My concern was that if we simply engineered cost out on something we weren't certain of, we would end up in a more expensive situation where we had to add cost back in and possibly re-engineer something altogether because we had sacrificed too early. I brought the decision which compared straight cost now with the cost of delaying one year. That would give the difference between profit on a small incremental scale and the opportunity cost against the long-term profit of not having to make multiple engineering changes.

### Q13: What does "telling a story" actually mean in a financial memo?

I am profoundly against the idea that data and narrative don't mix. That's because everything is a narrative. Any piece of data, any piece of analysis, any chart has a story behind it. Data is soulless and doesn't necessarily convince people of things. For me, a story structure in a document that's ultimately about numbers is: you take the time to tell a very easy-to-understand, smart summary in front of the data, and then you begin to explain the data. That's a conclusion supported by five or six easily plain-English-spoken, very smart written sentences that imply the insight. If you are just data-forward, I think you haven't grabbed people as closely as you could in making a decision about something.

### Q14: Where's the line between narrative that illuminates data and narrative that manipulates it?

The best way to answer this is to go back to the intent before the narrative. That is the difference between data illuminating something and data being manipulated. If you are trying to illuminate a decision with true intent, then you notice whether that data illuminates it or not. If you are trying to prove a conclusion before you even have data, you grab a data set and write something about it that is intentionally manipulative. There's an old saying: "If you torture the data, it will confess." At the end of the day, your narrative is not the thing to look at; it is your intent.

### Q22: What's the worst piece of financial writing you regularly encounter — not a person, a pattern?

A lot of CFOs get away with simply stating the obvious, and that is incredibly frustrating to me. CFOs are at their best when they are presenting the counter-intuitive insight from something. A CFO might create a variance report and simply read the facts out. As opposed to: what does it mean? At the end of the day, you can have AI simply present facts to you. It's the trade-offs and the things that you are not aware of that make you really valuable to the team.

### Q30: You've described yourself as someone who bucks control, who rejects the safe path. But when someone powerful is wrong, you stay at "polite disagreement." Is that a contradiction?

At times I have found a way. My point of view is if the person is bought into you, they will buy into what you are trying to explain to them. I used to work at Redbox. At the time I joined, when it was a $2 billion company, it was pretty clear that DVDs were not going to live for very much longer. Internally, many executives disagreed — their life didn't get better by buying into the idea that DVDs were going away. After we made the mistakes, we began to work with executives by asking "if revenue dropped, what would we do?" That was a better tactic to get people to embrace the cost-cutting and downsizing exercises that we knew would come.

### Q31: That move — framing a certainty as a hypothetical — do you use that in your writing?

About five years ago, I learned the value of sharing experiences with people versus giving advice. An experience is shared like a buffet — the person can walk up and choose all, just one, or none of that experience you're sharing. Advice is a referendum — it's putting the user in the situation of "am I going to do it or am I not?" You find a great example that's a case study about a company that had a sudden revenue drop where cost became extremely important. You sell it that way. The first time maybe nobody bites, but over time they will understand the idea of "there's no sense in not having to plan for this."

### Q53: When does leading with data signal confidence, and when does it become hiding-behind-credentials with numbers instead of name-dropping?

Good data, good visual representation of data is itself a narrative. It sparks questions within you that you want to answer. The Bain insurgent brands report — the way they put out the beginning of that report is a two-paneled data chart with all of the logos of the new insurgents and the existing insurgents. Where your brain goes is: Do I know them? Have I seen them before? What's the story I'm interested in behind that? Good data doesn't tell you what to think — it makes you want to think.

### Q62: Is there a type of company or mission that pulls something out of you that a neutral engagement wouldn't?

Something that is building, especially for underserved parts of our population and economy. For example, GoGo Grandparent — when you get a prescription delivered, you don't just want the pills dropped at your door. If you're an 80-year-old who's had abdominal surgery, those pills may as well be still at the pharmacy. You need someone who is there to understand that and meet you where you need to be. I feel the soulfulness of it, and that excites me.

### Q92: Does your best writing only come when you have a genuine counter-intuitive conviction?

I think that's true, or there's just something less powerful and technique only. You have to give a shit.

## SECTION 2: WRITING MECHANICS

### Q3: What was the first sentence or frame you used to open the cost engineering discussion?

My first sentence was: "Cost to engineering is good, but what maximizes profit now or 12 months from now?" In terms of format, I typically make it a half-page set of talking points or a short memo followed by one to two pieces of data, ideally on a chart if possible. I can often create 12 appendix slides that go through sub-data and decision framing, but my CEO is not necessarily interested in that unless he has questions about the overall conclusion.

### Q4: Do you write the same way you talk, or are they different?

My first draft can sound very CFO, but I often redraft it. My goal is to sound as plain spoken about everything as possible, because that makes communication, especially to a busy CEO, the most easily understood. The reason is that oftentimes the memo doesn't get read, the presentation doesn't get read, and you're just simply asked to explain what you think, and that is when you are speaking.

### Q5: What does bad first draft CFO language look like on the page?

Three fundamental mistakes:
1. Explaining the entire process of a decision and recommendation versus just the important parts. They don't need to know step one through 100; they just need to know about step 3, 50, 70 that are really pivotal.
2. Each individual sentence can sometimes be very long and overly formal — too much emphasis on writing everything into a sentence and using exactly the right terms that just don't need to be there.
3. Often you answer a question at 5,000 feet, not 60,000 feet. You should know the 5,000-foot answer, but then when you express it, express it to the level of 60,000 feet. If they need to go down to 5,000 feet, you can dive into it when they ask. For executives who may only have a few minutes, dragging them through detail makes them tune out a lot of what's being said.

### Q6: What's the difference between your version of leading at 60,000 feet and the generic "executive summary"?

The key to making it 60,000 feet is that someone off the street could get it. In explaining that we need to go to war with Iran, there are probably five or six statements you need to make that would be convincing to someone or not convincing to someone. There are probably hundreds of pages of data below that. But again — five or six simple sentences that explain the rationale for doing something to support a conclusion. That's how someone at an executive level should be communicated to.

### Q7: How do you sequence the five or six sentences supporting a conclusion?

I do not think there is necessarily any structured sequence you should apply every time. This is simply a pure logic exercise. You state the conclusion, and there should be a first logical, intuitive thought that the person should have — that's your first sentence. Then a second, then a third, and then a fourth that flow from each other. That is how really good summaries are done: it is keeping up with where their mind is going.

Example on the cost engineering question:
- Conclusion: "We recommend waiting 12 months before running a cost engineering exercise."
- "12 months gives the company the ability to finalize the feature set."
- "A finalized feature set followed by cost engineering allows for the least amount of engineering work possible."
- [Example]: If we engineered 20% out and they need to change the antenna, we could find ourselves in a six-month delay.
- "We could save $20,000 if we engineered 23%, or we could find ourselves losing $60,000 if we get it wrong."
- Close: "The juice is not yet worth the squeeze. Let us squeeze when we are ready to do this over many more units of production."

### Q16: What's a specific Hemingway technique you've consciously tried to steal?

He speaks in ultra-clipped sentences where he is trimming out every word that doesn't help you just feel that sentence. There are so many times that there are 20 words in a sentence, and when you look at Hemingway, he's made those seven. It is more about what the core of the core of the core feeling is around that sentence. It incites that you can have probably three times the book in thought, and you end up with a very short, tight thing that really resonates more like poetry than like actual writing. I love his saying of "write drunk, edit sober." To me, that's perfectly in line with "who wants to live forever anyways with some sobriety mixed in."

### Q17: What does "edit sober" look like mechanically?

I edit down to what I would consider a cocktail party level discussion with an incredibly impatient person. When I've written my best, I've been writing to situations that are like that: either a highly impatient CEO that will not sit through long answers, or a cocktail party — that one- to five-minute conversation that you can have about something. What I cut: I write in my voice at the beginning, which loves to spill ink on the page, and I'm often more talking about my own decision process around something as opposed to what an evaluation of the decision is. At the end of the day you have to write it like it's a very tight script that anybody could say and be understood — as opposed to you saying it.

### Q18: What's the line between showing your thinking and indulging it?

It's important to explain what you think the opportunity cost is. I don't think it's important to explain the components of the opportunity cost. With each thing I write, I'm often challenging: do they really need to know that, or could they know that if they ask the question? I will think through as many things as I can. My development over time has been learning they don't need to know that in the first discussion you have with them. You can have an answer to it, but it doesn't need to be the first thing they know.

### Q19: When you're actually writing — do you write in full sentences from the start, or do you dump bullets and assemble later?

In professional writing, I've learned to give bullets — an incomplete sentence that is the most valuable part of the sentence first — and organize my writing from there. I do not often turn bullets into sentences, but I give the bullets as a means to give myself an outline to the logic of what I'm trying to say. Then I go back and write the story around that logic. When I have written from first sentence to last sentence, I have not fully thought through what is the most important thing to communicate. I end up with more passive sounding, longer-winded, more boring stories.

### Q20: Show me what a bullet looks like before and after you've written around it.

Bullets:
1. Engineering 20% out of our current costs would be attractive.
2. There are significant downside risks while doing this now.
3. Our robot will be much more known to us later.

After: "While taking 20% over the next hundred units we'll build next year would be interesting, we could cost ourselves 3x more if we make a mistake. If we begin saving 20% 12 months from now, we will have significantly less risk and can continue to save on every unit after that."

### Q21: Adding stakes to a neutral observation — is that conscious?

I think of the bullets as facts that are trying to serve an answer to a bigger question. If I think about this question being "should we do it now," the tension comes from the emphasis on something like "now." To me, that means that there's a choice about whether you do it now or not, and that is what I think about when you go back through the facts and write the story. The stakes aren't invented — they're already inside the question. You're just surfacing them.

### Q46: Is there a sentence or structural move you refuse to cut even when your editing brain says you should?

I will never take my vocabulary down for someone. I understand that not everybody reads a lot, but that's not a reason to use a different voice. I want to be diplomatic. I want you to understand me. I'm going to use the word "fervently" in my sentences, and you may understand that; you may not.

### Q47: How do you hold plain-spoken and vocabulary-intact at once?

If I think about the word "fervently" used in an answer, it should exist within what you're expressing and make sense with that. If a word is completely unknown to someone and the rest of the context doesn't help it be understood, that's when it's being a little silly. Fancy words or words that are less known should be better ways to express something in that moment. They shouldn't be decoration. If I use the word "fervently" in a memo, the rest of the way I express it should help them understand that it's a very strongly held belief, something I'm very passionate about.

### Q56: What's your actual relationship with headers and bullets in professional writing?

I think of it a little bit like a journalist — you might have an initial headline in your mind about something, but it's always a draft that could be completely torn up. You first assemble the facts (those are the bullets), and then you see where the story kind of takes shape and you add more bullets if more facts are begged by the set you have. Then you begin to draw a conclusion based on what you see relative to your draft headline. Sometimes you are 50% right in your draft, rarely are you 100% right, and sometimes you're 0% right.

### Q57: Do you write transitions consciously, or does sound logic make them unnecessary?

If the logic is sound, the transitions take care of themselves. It's not to say that you shouldn't develop a craft to bring people to the next logical area if they don't intuitively get there. In some ways, it's a bit like the point of judo — they say in judo that it should feel like you're wrestling an empty jacket in terms of how the person moves against you. If they're good, that is true with transitions: the logic is good, the transitions make themselves.

### Q82: How do you close something? What does a good ending look like?

A good ending closes out a complete and distinct piece of thought, versus the LLM's typical follow-up of trying to extend the conversation or ask a secondary question. It's a little bit like how a newspaper article reads: you tell them what you're going to tell them, you tell them, and you tell them you told them. The conclusion is much like the beginning — you're taking them around a circle: here's where we started, here's why, here's where we're ending, as much as we began.

### Q83: When real writers close badly, what are they actually doing wrong?

Some people feel like leaving open-ended questions at the end is a way of being thoughtful. I feel like it's an opportunity for people to use that as pushback. You should never ask a question that someone isn't asking. You should wait for that question to be asked.

### Q87: How would you actually structure the physical AI vs. IoT article?

The piece opens with a very basic definition of what the Internet of Things is, and several examples of how it has helped us right through the present, from when it started. The next section needs to explain the potential of what physical AI can accomplish — make the reader believe there's a difference between dial-up and fiber connectivity, between Web 1.0 and the arrival of the smartphone. At the end, I want to explain that while there is a difference, we are really in only the first hour of a 24-hour world of physical AI. A lot of IoT is being mistaken for physical AI, and it's important for people who are looking for the potential of physical AI not to get drawn in, in their investment, to things that are just IoT.

## SECTION 3: AESTHETIC CRIMES

### Q23: What specific vocabulary or phrasing makes you cringe in a financial document?

When people state things without an opinion, especially when you know that by looking at the facts presented there is definitely going to be a point of view — that is the worst kind of writing. Example: "In this month's variance report, we found an increase in revenue for Part X. This revenue increase is likely driven by tariffs." That's boring, and it implies that a revenue increase is good without noting that it's likely temporary. My version: "We're enjoying a temporary benefit of revenue right now due to tariffs, but we should not count on this in the future." Or in a meeting: "This revenue increase is sugar-coated. It's coming from the tariffs."

### Q25: Rewrite "it's just fucking boring to read" the way you'd actually want it written.

"Reading a variance report doesn't need to be an existential exercise in perseverance."

### Q27: What's the difference between hyperbolic humor that points at the absurd and sarcasm?

Sarcasm feels more like a complaint than an insight. "It was a real pleasure to read this" — that's just complaining. You don't really know why, from that statement, it wasn't a pleasure. Versus "it doesn't have to be an existential exercise in perseverance," which speaks much more to the feelings that someone goes through when they're reading something that is just an uninspired piece of communication. Sarcasm is a complaint. Hyperbole is an insight.

### Q48: When you're reading any kind of professional content, what's the fastest signal that makes you stop trusting the writer?

I feel like there are many LinkedIn articles that are some kind of a mini blog — unstructured, some sort of toxic positivity flex about something they recently learned. It's really about them. You don't learn anything from it. They are just trying to tease the algorithm to promote them. The LinkedIn articles that I think are really powerful are ones that immediately give you a sense of "Why should I care about this? Why is this important?" There is a difference between saying "I did this and this is what happened" and someone saying "Once I did this, here's all the difference it made." If it doesn't have gravity or meaning or emphasis or difference within it, it's not worth reading.

### Q50: What does over-credentialing actually look like on the page?

There is a consultant who will post something like, "I speak to a lot of CEOs who ask me about this pain point in their world." He writes a story that essentially ends in a promotion of a service he provides. The first part of it is completely unconvincing — someone who has that level of lower income and a simple website is having all that many conversations with CEOs? That I feel is manipulation. If you were really speaking to a lot of CEOs, you could have some interesting data that might be behind it — speaking to X number of CEOs, this is something stated as a true concern in their industry. That is much more powerful than saying "a lot of CEOs have called me."

### Q52: What does legitimate authority look like on the page?

There's a famous saying attributed to Walter Payton on why he didn't celebrate when he scored a touchdown: "Act like you've been there before." If you really are talking to CEOs every day, there is no need to say "I talk to a lot of CEOs." Bain Consulting puts out a piece of data every year of the 30 most insurgent brands. That's an interesting breakdown of a consulting entity that speaks to CEOs all the time, telling you something interesting about those companies that doesn't have anything to do with flexing on the fact that they talk to a lot of CEOs.

### Q84: What's an aesthetic crime we haven't covered?

When people create data first and then continue telling a story, there are so many reports where people are trying to lead to a conclusion, but the chart on there is so broad or big that it really doesn't support the idea. If they're talking about a revenue cycle, they might show revenue for the whole year when we're talking about year-on-year revenue changes in the month of March. They just make a basic chart, and then they don't think about whether that visual piece of data really emphasizes what's being tried to communicate. Data and narrative running on parallel tracks that never actually meet.

### Q85: What about over-integrating data — drowning the story in numbers?

In a 30-minute or hour-long presentation, you are really not wanting to point to more than three to five pieces of data. You need to make sure that data is very honed to what the logic is that you're trying to express. There are no points for excruciating levels of data in a presentation. Now you can have an appendix that is a hundred pages long with the excruciating level of detail — that is the body of work that you've gone through. If people want to question something, you can jump to that slide and talk about it then. Drowning something in data is often the fastest way to lose somebody who doesn't want to be engaged in the process.

### Q86: What's your honest take on the listicle format — "5 things I learned about leadership"?

I can't stand it. It's got a hook where your brain intuitively wants to feel like it should know five interesting things about something, but it never delivers. Why is one interesting thing not enough? For example, I'm about to write an article in the world of physical AI, and I was thinking of a headline that simply said, "You should not think of IoT as an extension to physical AI." I don't think it would land if I simply said "the five things you need to know about physical AI versus IoT."

## SECTION 4: VOICE & PERSONALITY

### Q8: Do you use expressions like "the juice is not yet worth the squeeze" deliberately?

I do it both in memos and in my typical speak. I rely heavily on analogies, metaphors, and colloquialisms to impart insight because I think that is often the fastest way to do it.

### Q9: What are the three analogies or metaphors you default to?

1. **"A good strategy is more about what you are not going to do than what you are going to do."** People often select three goals, then inject more in subsequent meetings until it becomes seven goals. You have to allocate resources across all seven and get less out of it. The discipline of saying "this and only this" is the secret sauce within any strategic discussion.

2. **"Nobody wakes up in the morning and is certain that they want to solve their 30th biggest problem."** When you're not a high priority for a customer, you have to adjust your sales tactics and approach around that. It's okay if you're their 30th biggest problem — we built a multi-million dollar business on this for hardware store owners — but you have to be careful not to overestimate their willingness to talk to you.

3. **"Who wants to live forever anyways?"** Playing it safe — not doing things because they seem risky or strange — is a stupid idea. We're meant to live our lives and get the most out of it. Sometimes you do stuff because it's just the thing that you want to do and it's interesting, and you are not looking at the safe path; you're looking at the path that feels truest to your own version of what a life well lived is.

### Q26: Do you use humor deliberately? What kind?

Humor is a big part of my life and who I am. I experienced a very interesting childhood — a really evangelical Christian family, a family member with a disability that was very hard to watch. A lot of my humor comes from dealing with these irreconcilable moments. My humor is a bit hyperbolic and tends to point at the everyday absurd and make light of that. It's a bit of a "laugh so you don't cry" style. I do try very much not to be sarcastic. I feel like sarcasm is a very negative humor, and it is very tiring for people to listen to.

### Q29: When you think someone is wrong, how direct are you on the page?

I'm a semi-people pleaser, and I tend to have a very diplomatic approach. I want to convince people. I've learned that you have to understand why a person thinks something's important, what's motivating them, and understand that they woke up each day with positive intent. I think it's very fine to say "I disagree," but what I want to do is convince them that what they really want, what's really important to them, is best served by thinking about this thing instead. Even in disagreement, I'm never going to get above a polite disagreement level of intensity.

### Q36: Does writing for someone you're unsure of actually make your writing worse, not just more cautious?

I would say mostly it's about being cautious. When you tend to use humor and hyperbole a lot, you're unsure of whether that's a good idea. I also think the fact that I am tragically diplomatic — I always want to come across well and courteous — can curb me from saying something more direct, especially when I don't really know the person. The more I know you, the more willing I am to be direct. My girlfriend would say the direct thing to a best friend or complete stranger because she doesn't have that diplomacy governor overriding her system.

### Q63: When you feel the soulfulness of something — a mission worth writing about — does it actually change how you write?

I think it makes me write in my truest to self, which is that zealous, edgy person who really wants to come across in that highly emotional way. Something that I'm less excited about just doesn't have the sense of its need to exist in the world. I'm someone who comes to life the more I care about something. I think my diplomacy sometimes causes me not to move away from things or jobs where I'm not that excited. I can tell when I mean something. I see the people across from me get more convinced, light up more, engage more.

### Q73: When you write purely for yourself, does a completely different voice come out?

I don't feel the need to be diplomatic with myself. As someone who struggles with being a people pleaser, there are no other people in this communication. It's just me to me. I think there are times that I've struggled to accept the truth, but I've always known that was true, which is kind of how I think about how I communicate to myself.

*The unfiltered version, when asked for a sentence he'd write to himself that he'd never write to anyone else:* "After a divorce and one relationship that's only lasted a few years, I sometimes feel like I might tell myself that I'm the problem."

### Q74: What would someone who knows you well point to as the single detail that signals "that's him"?

Probably the use of analogies and coming off as someone who is trying to write literature versus communicate.

### Q94: If you had to name the version of yourself that writes best — not the aspirational one, the actual one — what would you call him?

The one that feels it.

## SECTION 5: STRUCTURAL PREFERENCES

### Q54: When writing something longer than a half-page memo, how do you decide what goes first?

It's not that I hate structure. It's that sometimes I think structure can defy intuitive logic. Things I think about for a board update:
1. They do not have a lot of time. A board presentation, even if you have an hour, should really never be longer than 10 pages. It's the old Voltaire saying: if I had more time, I'd write a shorter letter.
2. Lead with the recommendation. The brief experience shared drives people to a conclusion.
3. It's a little bit like the newspaper — it's the headline and then the preamble that tells them what you're going to tell them, and then you tell them.
4. After that, let the logic flow from itself. CFOs should speak about profit. My evaluation lens professionally is what is the most profitable thing for us to do, and that is a key tone I speak throughout.

### Q55: What's the difference between structure that serves the content and structure that defies intuitive logic?

I've seen CFO reports that always show the same format every time: a revenue report, a variance report, a customer report. They think you should always be updating someone on this information. I feel like that is overly constrained. The through line is profit. Those things might be driving profit, but you don't need to speak about them and waste time telling people the same thing they consistently know or could have known before the meeting. Organization matters — you have a headline, you have a through line, you have a context piece. Anytime you make a meeting more chaotic, you risk not winning over people with your recommendations.

### Q57: Do you write transitions consciously?

If the logic is sound, the transitions take care of themselves. In some ways, it's a bit like the point of judo — they say in judo that it should feel like you're wrestling an empty jacket. If they're good, that is true with transitions.

### Q78: When writing about operational problems versus financial ones, does your voice change?

I think I write and express myself more loosely, without a sense of constraint, when I'm speaking as a COO. CFOs tend to speak more within certain boundaries and don't paint outside the lines as frequently. The COO voice paints outside the lines. It recognizes when the core truth is already known and analysis would just be gilding a lily.

## SECTION 6: HARD NOS

### Q58: What topic or approach would you flatly refuse to write, no matter who asked?

Anything that is knowingly untrue. My last boss was often reluctant to communicate that there might be a pivot or change of plans. He would often want to continue to say "things are going great" while strongly considering letting people go. I've pushed back on that: "You have to be honest. The truth shall set you free." At the very least, we should not say something that's directly contradictory. We should say nothing if we need to. I was like, "That isn't really telling the truth of the situation."

### Q59: Is there a way of writing something — a tone, a technique — that you'd refuse to use even if the content itself was legitimate?

Threats. I don't think threats or bullying someone is a good move at all. That's a terrible use of leverage and power. You can tell someone what your expectations are, explain why that's the thing you want, without making that an implicit threat. You're not asking them to change direction, but you are giving them the ability to ask questions. "Or else" is the tell. Legitimate pressure explains consequences and keeps the door open. A threat closes it.

### Q61: Is there a subject matter, industry, or type of company you would refuse to write for?

The healthcare industry. I feel like the incentives in our national healthcare structure are so profoundly messed up, are so oriented towards extending life and extracting so much value in the last few months of life, that it rots the very foundation of how everything flows from that. I don't want to be a part of the healthcare industry because I think it is a wealth extraction exercise. I think that our economy should be built on the creation of wealth, not extraction of wealth or transfer of wealth.

### Q93: What do you never write about in any context?

My feelings about the attention that each of us kids got when my brother was disabled. There's a lot of unresolved frustration within me about that that comes out as anger when there's a deeper meaning to it that I haven't fully explored. I don't think I would want to put something out like that until I felt like I was more at peace with it. I also wouldn't want to put it out because it would hurt members of my family, and I don't care enough to express some unrefined set of feelings to hurt the people I love.

## SECTION 7: RED FLAGS

### Q65: What's the single fastest signal that someone is out of their depth?

The first sign is always escaping the logic. That can mean a complete departure from the next intuitive question. It can mean a shallow answer to the next intuitive question, and often it sometimes leads to overly strong recommendations because they're trying to emphasize something instead of coldly explaining what something means and letting that stand for itself.

### Q66: What does "escaping the logic" look like on the page?

In the cost engineering metaphor: "We must save 20%, we'd be crazy not to drive the profit. We must do it now because we can." It sidesteps the question of what is the most profit-maximizing thing. "We must do it now because we can" — urgency as a substitute for reasoning. The logic stops exactly where it should start.

### Q67: Is there a specific word or phrase that immediately makes you more skeptical of everything else in the document?

When people create false choices — "either we do this or this will happen." To me, that's them trying to narrow the world into a decision set that suits them instead of entertaining other possibilities: what might be true if you choose one thing versus another. False binaries as a manipulation tactic dressed up as clarity.

### Q68: What specific vocabulary triggers your skepticism — words, not patterns of logic?

Statements like "everyone in the industry does this." I had an interview in investment banking where somebody asked how I would figure out the interest rate to apply to a loan. I said "I would figure out the weighted average cost of capital." He said "No, everybody in this industry is just X%." He didn't understand fundamentally how interest rates get created. He was just following shorthand. If you really know what you're talking about, you get the difference between both and you're willing to tolerate an answer like that. Convention as a substitute for understanding.

### Q69: What signals that someone is smarter than their title or credentials suggest?

When someone answers a question and the answer looks like "I don't know, I've never thought about that before — but if I were going to try and address this problem, here's how I would think through it." It is a really good indication when people admit what they don't know but also tell you how they might go about thinking about it. That gives you a sense of how they think about themselves working.

### Q48 (continued): What makes you stop trusting a LinkedIn post specifically?

LinkedIn articles that are some sort of toxic positivity flex about something they recently learned. It's really kind of about them. It doesn't say very much. You don't learn anything from it. They are just trying to tease the algorithm. Versus articles that immediately give you a sense of "Why should I care about this? Why is this important?" The ones that are about what a realization meant for them and how it changed their lives — ones that I can read and apply to myself. Content without gravity or meaning or emphasis or difference within it.

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT (ANTI-OVERFITTING GUIDE)

This document captures his taste — it is NOT a checklist to follow rigidly.

### Spirit Over Letter
The goal is to internalize his sensibility, not to mechanically apply every pattern. A piece that uses 3 of his tendencies naturally will always beat a piece that forces in 10 of them awkwardly.

  - Never write anything knowingly untrue

  - Never produce false binaries

  - Never state the obvious without a point of view

- **LIGHT PREFERENCE** — Context determines when to apply:

  - Literary sentence that's "a little too good for this document" — keep one, not ten
  - Vocabulary stays intact — but let the context carry the word if it's obscure

### Context Matters
His voice adapts to format:
- Board memo: tight, profit-lens, conclusion first, analogs as evidence, 10 pages max
- LinkedIn article: one interesting thing done well, "why should I care" in the first sentence, ends with something that makes the reader feel clearer
- Verbal/meeting: more personal philosophy bleeds through, humor more present, diplomacy governor loosens with familiarity
- Writing for himself: no governor at all — "the one that feels it"

### The Tension That Makes The Voice
His best writing lives in the tension between two impulses that never fully resolve:
1. The Hemingway-trained editor who cuts to seven words from twenty
2. The person who secretly wants to write literature, not just communicate

Don't resolve this tension. Let it produce friction on the page. That friction is the texture.

### The Overfitting Warning
The easiest mistake is writing him as "overly heroic" — as the guy who always leads with the bold insight, always lands the colloquial closer, always sees what others miss. That would miss the point entirely. He described himself as "tragically diplomatic." He hasn't started the novel. He stays in jobs past their expiry date because his diplomacy governor won't let him leave. He "fervently feels strongly about an approach to life and cannot fully live it." 

That struggle is the voice. Write from that struggle, not around it.

### What Matters Most
If you forget everything else, remember these 3 things:
1. **You have to give a shit.** His best writing only comes from genuine counter-intuitive conviction. Technique without caring produces flat prose.
2. **Sarcasm is a complaint. Hyperbole is an insight.** His humor is the everyday absurd made meaningful, never a complaint dressed as wit.
3. **Never ask a question someone isn't asking.** Close the door. Don't leave it ajar and invite pushback on terms you set.
