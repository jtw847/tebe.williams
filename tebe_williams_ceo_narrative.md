# Tebe Williams — CEO Narrative

The next decade belongs to companies that put AI in the world, not just on a screen.

Most of the companies racing to win in Physical AI will lose for the same reason. They are building the greatest version of what they imagine the product will be. By the time it ships, the market has moved, the customer has changed their mind, and 90% of what was built turns out to be irrelevant. I have watched this happen. I have also watched the alternative: a company that builds what the customer actually needs, proves the thesis cheaply, and scales the thing that is working. That company wins.

I have spent 25 years building the alternative.

---

**Rebuilding Who the Customer Was**

When I joined VS Networks, the business had one customer and less than $500,000 in revenue. The product was real. The market was not. My first move was not to optimize what existed. It was to find out who actually had the problem we were solving. We rebuilt the customer definition from scratch — moving from a single John Deere relationship into steel distributors, hardware chains, agricultural equipment companies, and commercial truck dealers, connected by a single adjacency insight about equipment dealerships. Revenue grew 700%. Customer churn stayed below 1%. When we sold, the acquirer paid 8.3x revenue — the highest multiple they had ever paid. They attributed it directly to what they found in customer diligence.

---

**Building Before Anyone Asked**

At Outerwall, I identified that Coinstar's most profitable long-term dollar was not in Redbox. It was in Europe. I built the development case, recruited the pilot stores, secured the operating partners, and hired the initial team. Nobody asked me to. It was the right move. Coinstar Europe now represents 40% of company revenues.

---

**Operating at the Edge of the Internet**

Physical AI does not operate in a clean environment. It operates at the edge of the internet, in extreme weather, with no staff on site and customers who have no patience for downtime. At Volley, I built a national deployment of autonomous AI-enabled robotic systems from zero to more than 200 installations, operating 24 hours a day, seven days a week, coast to coast. We solved the latency problem by running redundant high-processing compute inside each unit. We solved the support problem by engineering the machine to be repairable by club staff without a technician present. We solved the economics problem by using equipment financing instead of equity to fund expansion. Customers describe Volley's support as extraordinary. That is not an accident. It is a system.

---

**The Mistake I Will Fix First**

Physical AI companies tend to overbuild their initial product. The logic is that the development cycle is so long that you need to build something expansive enough to cover everything you might learn in the field. What that logic skips is the step where you actually find out what the customer values. There is almost always a simpler, earlier version of the product that proves the thesis before you spend millions of dollars and extra months building something the customer never asked for. The greatest version of what you imagine the product will be is usually 10% relevant. I start with the 10%.
